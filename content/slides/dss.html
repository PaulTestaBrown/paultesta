Our argument

The personal is political

  • Citizens learn about politics through their personal experiences

    • Policy feedback (Campbell 2012; Lerman and McCabe 2017; Weaver, Prowse, and Piston 2019);
    • Deliberative democracy (Mansbridge 1999; Cramer Walsh 2004)
    • Political knowledge (Cramer and Toff 2017)

  • Personal narratives can be a powerful rhetorical frame

    • Narrative frames are easier to understand (Bilandzic and Busselle 2013)

    • Episodic structure more emotionally engaging (Gross 2008)

Abortion is an “Easy Issue”

  • Abortion is an “Easy Issue” … in that it’s not hard for people to form strong opinions (Carmines and Stimson 1980)

    • Gender, religion, partisanship, core values, … (Jelen and Wilcox 2003)

  • Persuasion on abortion should be difficult

    • People are more likely to change one’s vote (Arceneaux 2008) or partisanship (Carsey and Layman 2006)

Abortion is an “Easy Issue”

  • Abortion is an “Easy Issue” … in that it’s not hard for people to form strong opinions (Carmines and Stimson 1980)

    • Gender, religion, partisanship, core values, … (Jelen and Wilcox 2003)

  • Persuasion on abortion should be difficult

    • People are more likely to change one’s vote (Arceneaux 2008) or partisanship (Carsey and Layman 2006)

  • Yet aggregate stability can mask individual change

    • Most people are ambivalent, holding a mix of pro-choice and pro-life views (Cook, Wilcox, and Jelen 2019)

Why personal narratives might persuade

  • Some evidence from observational studies that personal narratives

    • Reduce stigma (Cockrill and Biggs 2018)
    • Elicit more positive comments on YouTube (Kubin et al. 2021)

  • But experimental results are more mixed

    • Null results from:
      • Deep canvassing (Broockman, Kalla, and Sekhon 2017)
      • Narrative stories at Planned Parenthood (Sackeim et al. 2020)
    • Effects for individually tailored arguments (Kalla, Levine, and Broockman 2022)

Persuasion and choice

  • Zaller (1992) Receive-Accept-Sample model of opinion formation

    • Attitude formation depends on both the reception and response to information

    • Typically only study one aspect of this dynamic

  • Dynamics of choice and self-selection are ever-present in everyday life, but absent in most studies of persuasion

  • When reception predicts response, this is a problem

How should we study the effects of sharing personal experiences on attitudes about abortion?

The standard approach:

  • Want to know the effect of a message?

  • Do an experiment!

The problem

  • Average Treatment Effects (\(ATE\)s) tell us the effect if everyone were to receive the treatment

  • We can think of it as a weighted average of the effects among those likely and unlikely to encounter the treatment

  • When treatment effects vary depending on who receives the message, the \(ATE\) can be misleading

    • Example: The same message delivered on Fox News vs. MSNBC

The problem

People likely to hear stories about abortion may be more receptive or may have already internalized the message

Likely: +/0

The problem

The same message may have no effect, may elicit backlash, or might persuade an unlikely audience.

Likely: +/0

Unlikely: -/0/+

The problem

The \(ATE\) we estimate from a standard design depends on the relative size and response of these groups

Likely: +

ATE: 0

Unlikely: -

Important

A polarizing treatment can yield a null \(ATE\) even when it produces large but opposing effects across audiences

The solution

  • Give some people the choice whether to receive the treatment

  • Preference-Incorporating Choice and Assignment design (PICA) (De Benedictis-Kessner et al. 2019)

The solution

PICA designs with a binary choice allow us to decompose the \(ATE\) into the Average Choice-Specific Treatment Effects (\(ACTE\)s) among a message’s likely and unlikely audiences (Gaines and Kuklinski 2011; Knox et al. 2019)

Expectations

  • H1: Personal narratives reach a broader audience than generalized accounts

\[ATE_{H1} = E[\alpha_{\text{Personal}} \mid C=p, D=c] - E[\alpha_{\text{General}} \mid C=g, D=c]\]

Expectations

  • H2: Personal narratives are more effective in changing abortion attitudes than more generalized arguments

\[ATE_{\text{H2}} = E[Y \mid T=p, D=f] - E[Y \mid T=g, D=f]\]

Expectations

  • H3: The effects of each narrative depend on the likelihood of engaging with them.

    – H3a: Personal narratives persuade both their likely audience and those who might otherwise avoid them

\[ ACTE_{Likely} = \frac{ \overbrace{E[Y|D=c, C=p] }^{\text{Average: Given Choice}} - \overbrace{E[Y|D=f,T=c]}^{\text{Average: Assigned Control}}} {\underbrace{\alpha}_{\text{Proportion Choosing to Read Personal Narrative}}} \]

\[ ACTE_{Unlikely} = \frac{ \overbrace{E[Y|D=f,T=p]}^{\text{Average: Assigned Personal}} - \overbrace{E[Y|D=c, C = p] }^{\text{Average: Given Choice}}} {\underbrace{(1-\alpha)}_{\text{Proportion Avoiding Personal Narrative}}} \]

Expectations

  • H3: The effects of each narrative depend on the likelihood of engaging with them.

    – H3b: General arguments persuade their likely audience but may induce backlash among those who would avoid these arguments.

\[ ACTE_{Likely} = \frac{ \overbrace{E[Y|D=c, C=g] }^{\text{Average: Given Choice}} - \overbrace{E[Y|D=f,T=c]}^{\text{Average: Assigned Control}}} {\underbrace{\alpha}_{\text{Proportion Choosing to Read General Argument}}} \]

\[ ACTE_{Unlikely} = \frac{ \overbrace{E[Y|D=f,T=g]}^{\text{Average: Assigned General}} - \overbrace{E[Y|D=c, C = g] }^{\text{Average: Given Choice}}} {\underbrace{(1-\alpha)}_{\text{Proportion Avoiding General Argument}}} \]

Power analyses

  • Summary
  • H1
  • H2
  • H3 – Low
  • H3 – High

With a total sample size of 1,500 (split between forced exposure and treatment choice conditions), our design is powered at 80% (with a two-tailed \(\alpha = 0.05\)) to detect the following minimum effect sizes:

  • H1: 0.10
  • H2: 0.25
  • H3:
    • 0.70 when one-third of respondents select into treatment
    • 0.35 when two-thirds of respondents select into treatment

Results

Summary

  • Personal narratives reach a broader audience than general arguments (H1)

    • No single factor predicts who engages with a message

  • Personal narratives are effective at changing abortion attitudes…

  • …but only among respondents who would otherwise avoid such stories (H2/H3)

  • General arguments are generally unpersuasive (H2/H3)

Personal narratives reach a broader audience than more general arguments

No single factor predicts who engages with a message

Implications, limitations, and broader applications

Implications

  • Reading a personal narratives about abortion is persuasive

  • But primarily among those who would otherwise avoid them

    • Standard experimental designs – even with subgroup analyses – would miss these dynamics

  • Understanding persuasion requires us to study both the reception and response to messages

Limitations

  • It is difficult to perfectly recreate the choices people face

    • Binary choice vs. multiple options
    • Some choices are made for us

  • Just because this personal narrative was persuasive doesn’t mean all narratives are

  • Use caution when interpreting results from a single study

    • Duration of effects, competing messages, different messengers, different contexts

Broader applications

  • Incorporating choice into experimental designs allows us to ask and answer a broader range of questions of interest to academics, activists, organizations and anyone trying to reach a particular or multiple audiences.

  • Once you bring choice into a design, there’s so much more you can do:

    • Randomize the choice people get
      • Binary choice vs. multiple options
      • Messenger and format
    • Randomize the information provided
      • Pro vs con
      • Expected vs unexpected messages
    • Randomize conditional on choice
      • Could a different message persuade a skeptical audience?
